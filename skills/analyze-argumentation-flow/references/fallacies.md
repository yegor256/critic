# Fallacy Catalogue

Consult this catalogue when naming a specific fallacy in a
  finding; quote the line that triggers the call and use the
  name below verbatim so the reader can look it up.

**Ad hominem** — attacking the author rather than the
  argument, or dismissing a claim because of who made it.

**Straw man** — restating the opponent's position in a
  weaker form than they hold it, then defeating the weaker
  version.

**False dichotomy** — presenting two options as exhaustive
  when a third (or fourth) is available.

**Slippery slope** — claiming that one step inevitably
  leads to a chain of further steps without showing each
  link in the chain.

**Hasty generalization** — drawing a universal conclusion
  from a sample too small or too biased to support it.

**Post hoc ergo propter hoc** — concluding that because B
  followed A, A caused B.

**Appeal to authority** — citing an authority outside the
  authority's domain, or treating citation as proof rather
  than as evidence.

**Appeal to popularity** — arguing that a claim is true
  because many people believe it.

**Appeal to tradition** — arguing that a practice is
  correct because it has long been done that way.

**Appeal to novelty** — arguing that a practice is correct
  because it is new.

**Tu quoque** — deflecting a criticism by pointing out that
  the critic is guilty of the same fault.

**Red herring** — introducing an irrelevant point that
  diverts attention from the argument under debate.

**No true Scotsman** — defending a universal claim by
  redefining the category to exclude any counterexample.

**Composition** — inferring that a whole has a property
  because its parts have it.

**Division** — inferring that a part has a property because
  the whole has it.

**False analogy** — treating an illustrative analogy as if
  it proved the conclusion, when the two cases differ on
  the point that matters.

**Begging the question** — assuming the conclusion inside
  one of the premises, so the argument only convinces a
  reader who already agrees.

**Moving the goalposts** — raising the standard of
  evidence after the original standard has been met, so the
  argument can never be settled.
