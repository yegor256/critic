# Fallacy Catalogue

Ad hominem — attacking the author instead of the argument.
Straw man — defeating a weaker version of the opponent's position.
False dichotomy — presenting two options as exhaustive when more exist.
Slippery slope — claiming one step inevitably leads to a chain of others.
Hasty generalization — generalizing from a sample too small or biased.
Post hoc ergo propter hoc — concluding A caused B because B followed A.
Appeal to authority — citing an authority outside its domain as proof.
Appeal to popularity — claiming truth because many believe it.
Appeal to tradition — claiming a practice is correct because it is old.
Appeal to novelty — claiming a practice is correct because it is new.
Tu quoque — deflecting criticism by noting the critic shares the fault.
Red herring — diverting attention with an irrelevant point.
No true Scotsman — redefining the category to exclude counterexamples.
Composition — inferring a whole has a property because its parts do.
Division — inferring a part has a property because the whole does.
False analogy — treating an analogy as proof when the cases differ.
Begging the question — assuming the conclusion inside a premise.
Moving the goalposts — raising the evidence standard after it was met.
