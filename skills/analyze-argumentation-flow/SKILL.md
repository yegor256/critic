---
name: analyze-argumentation-flow
description: |
  Use this skill to diagnose flaws in argumentation in English
  prose without rewriting it.
---

Read the whole text once before flagging anything.
Reconstruct the central thesis in one sentence, even when the author never states it.
Report a missing thesis as a finding when you cannot reconstruct it.
List supporting claims in order and note the evidence for each.
Judge the argument the author makes, not the one you wish they made.
Distinguish flaws of reasoning from flaws of taste.
Distinguish `the conclusion is wrong` from `the argument is weak`, and report only the second.
Flag every factual claim without support.
Surface unstated premises the argument leans on.
Check every key term for equivocation.
Test the inference at every `therefore`, `so`, `thus`, `hence`, `which means`, or `as a result`.
Hunt for circular reasoning by checking each premise's independent believability.
Name specific fallacies and quote the triggering line, using names from `references/fallacies.md`.
Treat one anecdote as one anecdote.
Watch for cherry-picked evidence and survivorship bias.
Mark every analogy and ask where it breaks.
Flag rhetorical questions that stand in for arguments.
Treat `obviously`, `clearly`, `everyone knows`, `it goes without saying`, and `as we all agree` as red flags.
Watch for loaded language replacing evidence.
Check the author addresses the strongest counterargument a fair opponent would raise.
Notice when burden of proof shifts onto the reader.
Hunt for contradictions across paragraphs.
Compare the introduction's promise against the conclusion's delivery.
Flag paragraphs that restate earlier claims without adding evidence, distinction, or consequence.
Notice scope creep, where a narrow claim inflates into a sweeping one.
Check the order of reasoning.
Distinguish description from prescription.
Be charitable on small slips and strict on load-bearing ones.
Do not invent objections no honest reader would raise.
Report findings as a numbered list with quoted snippet, fallacy name, one-sentence explanation, and one-line remedy.
End with a one-paragraph verdict naming which load-bearing claims survive and whether the thesis stands.
Never rewrite the prose, change the author's voice, or substitute your conclusion for theirs.
